# What Expert Cardiologists Teach Us About AI Context

The diagnostic process that cardiologists use — pattern recognition, differential diagnosis, synthesis — is exactly what we should be engineering into AI memory systems.

## The Cardiology Parallel

When a cardiologist reads an ECG, they're not just pattern matching. They're integrating:

- **Current signal** (the ECG waveform)
- **Patient history** (prior conditions, medications, baselines)
- **Population data** (what this pattern means across thousands of cases)
- **Temporal context** (how this reading compares to the last one)

This is context architecture in action. The diagnosis isn't in any single piece of information — it's in the synthesis.

## What AI Systems Get Wrong

Most AI systems process information like a first-year medical student: they see the current input and try to match it to a pattern they memorized during training.

What they're missing is the integration layer — the ability to weigh current signals against historical context, population baselines, and temporal trends.

## Engineering Diagnostic Intelligence

To build AI systems with cardiologist-level context awareness, we need three architectural patterns:

### 1. Layered Memory
Not all context is equal. Recent interactions matter more than old ones. Domain-specific knowledge matters more than general knowledge. The memory system needs to reflect these priorities.

### 2. Differential Reasoning
Before committing to an answer, the system should generate alternative hypotheses and evaluate each against the available context. This is the AI equivalent of differential diagnosis.

### 3. Temporal Awareness
Context changes over time. What was true last month may not be true today. The system needs mechanisms for tracking context freshness and flagging stale information.

## The Takeaway

The best diagnostic systems — human or artificial — don't just retrieve information. They *synthesize* it. They hold multiple hypotheses simultaneously and let the evidence guide them to the right answer.

That's the standard we should be building toward.
