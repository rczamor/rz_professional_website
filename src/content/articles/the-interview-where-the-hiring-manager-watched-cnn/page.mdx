# The Interview Where the Hiring Manager Watched CNN

True story. I once had a video interview where the hiring manager spent the entire 45 minutes watching CNN on a second monitor.

I could see it in his eyes — the periodic flick to the left, the slight delay before responding, the way he'd ask me to repeat things that weren't complicated.

He wasn't present. And I knew within the first five minutes that I didn't want to work for someone who couldn't give a candidate 45 minutes of undivided attention.

## The Context of Attention

Here's the thing about attention: it's the most honest signal in any interaction. You can fake enthusiasm. You can rehearse questions. You can read from a scorecard. But you can't fake presence.

When someone is truly listening, you feel it. The conversation flows differently. The follow-up questions are sharper. The silences are comfortable instead of anxious.

## What This Taught Me About Leadership

In the years since, I've been on both sides of the table hundreds of times. And I've learned that the quality of attention you bring to a conversation is the quality of context you receive from it.

Leaders who half-listen get half-truths. They miss the subtext, the hesitation, the thing someone almost said but didn't. And then they wonder why they're always surprised.

## The Interview as a Context Problem

An interview is fundamentally a context exchange. Two parties are trying to build enough shared context to make a high-stakes decision.

When one party checks out — literally or figuratively — the context exchange collapses. You end up with a decision made on incomplete information. The hire might work out, but not because of the interview. Despite it.

## The Standard I Set

After that CNN interview, I made a rule for myself: every conversation gets my full attention. Not because it's polite (though it is), but because **attention is the first step in context architecture.**

You can't curate what you didn't capture. And you can't capture what you didn't notice.

The best leaders I've worked with share this trait. They're fully present. They remember details from conversations months ago. They connect dots that others miss.

It's not a superpower. It's a practice. And it starts with putting CNN on mute.
