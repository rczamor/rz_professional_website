# Building My Own Context Layer: Week 1

I've spent months writing about context architecture in theory. This week, I started building it for real.

## The Goal

Build a personal context layer — a system that captures, curates, synthesizes, and activates the information I encounter every day. Think of it as a second brain, but with an actual architecture instead of a pile of notes.

## Day 1-2: Capture

The first step was building the ingestion pipeline. Every article I read, every conversation I have, every idea that crosses my mind — it all needs a place to land.

I started simple: a Chrome extension that captures highlights with one click, and a Telegram bot for quick voice notes.

The lesson: **capture has to be frictionless or it doesn't happen.** If it takes more than 2 seconds, I skip it. And every skipped capture is lost context.

## Day 3-4: The Schema Problem

Here's where theory met reality. In my essays, I write about "structured knowledge representation." In practice, that means answering a surprisingly hard question: *What metadata do I attach to each piece of captured context?*

I landed on:
- **Source** (where did this come from?)
- **Domain** (what area of knowledge?)
- **Confidence** (how reliable is this?)
- **Connections** (what else does this relate to?)
- **Freshness** (when was this last validated?)

## Day 5-7: First Synthesis Attempt

By the end of the week, I had about 200 captured items. I wrote a simple script to cluster them by domain and surface connections.

The result was... humbling. The clusters were noisy, the connections were mostly spurious, and the "synthesis" was really just keyword matching wearing a trench coat.

## What I Learned

Building context architecture is harder than writing about it. The gap between "this should work in theory" and "this actually works in practice" is where the real engineering happens.

Next week: curation pipelines and the art of deciding what matters.
